General and minor tips

Unless it is a scientific article (which is going to be published somewhere), do not use “I” or “We”. Be impersonal: “It was decided....” “It could be hypothesized...” and so on.

Never refer to somebody who did the experiment as “subject”. Use instead “participant” (“subjects” is not used anymore for ethics reasons).

As it is something done in the past, always use simple past sentences. No Present Perfect!

English writing can be tricky for non-English speakers. Make sure you have short sentences, with a simple vocabulary. Please make sure you understand the comma rule (and in general put a comma before “and”).

When writing quantities, for example 57 cm or 12 seconds, it is important to add a space in between the number and the measure. For example, 57 cm and not 57cm.

Participants do not “have to” look at screen, discriminate etc. Instead, participants are “asked to” look at screen, discriminate etc.

Please write “PsychoPy” correctly (see capital letters), and add the following citation (if you are using it): Peirce, Jonathan W. “PsychoPy—psychophysics software in Python.” Journal of Neuroscience Methods 162.1-2 (2007): 8-13.

Abstract

How to write an abstract (follow this link and this one):

The abstract usually should be written at the end of all the writing process. If you do it at the end, it is also easier because you have clear in mind what the thesis is about, and you can “copy and paste” sentences into it from your Introduction and Discussion (not exactly a copy and paste, but close...).

Introduction

It is very important to have a clear structure in your thesis. If you are unsure about it, ask! It is much better to talk about these things at the beginning, rather than at the end. Even just talking for a few minutes can clear things in your head and speed up writing a lot (or at least this is what students said).

First write an outline, to get a clear idea of what you want to say. It can be a few sentences. Example of Introduction:

  1. Definition of XXX
  2. Where XXX was found
  3. What are XXX mechanisms?
  4. Where was XXX found?
  5. Why is it useful?

After writing the outline, start writing a paragraph for each point.

It is important to cite your supervisor during a thesis, but do not overdo it ;-) A good literature review, with a lot of publications clearly explained, is your best starting point to get a high grade! In case of doubts about concepts, feel free to ask questions. It is a very good sign when a student is interested in the topic and wants to do a good job ;-)

It is important to convey the aim of your project at the end of the Introduction. Several hypotheses can be formulated. Do not underestimate this part — it can be one sentence or two, but it is the core of your work. It is very useful to phrase your hypotheses with an If statement. Example:

IF we found xxx, then we would conclude that…
CONVERSELY, IF we found xxx, then we would conclude that…

This structure is very useful because you can refer to it in the Results and Discussion sections. Do not be afraid of rewriting parts, revising the structure etc, this is how writing usually goes and there is nothing shameful about it.

Method & Results

When you run an experiment or a statistical analysis, it is something that has necessarily happened in the past. Hence, use only past tenses!

Figures are the most important thing in a thesis. Everybody loves figures! Especially if they are easy to understand and make you understand the experiment right away. Keeping this in mind, put a lot of effort into creating them. No copy and paste — show that you have put effort into it. Take care of details (font size, figure quality, caption, colors etc). The secret to good writing is to attract and keep the attention of the reader (or the audience)! Sloppy figures put you right away in a terrible situation.

Do not forget to add pictures of your stimuli and design! It is very important that people get an idea of what was shown to participants.

Example structure of a figure explaining a trial:

Figure 1 (example caption for Procedure): Trial sequence. In each trial, participants were shown a group of computer-generated faces of the same identity varying mainly in their level of trustworthiness (Todorov et al., 2013). After an ISI of 250 ms, they were asked to adjust a randomly selected test face to match the average level of trustworthiness of the previously presented faces. After an ITI of 500 ms the next trial started.
Figure 2 (example caption for Results): The x-axis indicates the number of items presented on the screen on each trial. The y-axis indicates participants' average error relative to items presented on the screen. Each dot indicates the average response error for each set size condition. Error bars represent the standard error of the mean. Dashed lines indicate chance performance.

When having figures with the Results, please add “****” or “n.s.” to report the significance of your results IN the figure. There is nothing better than looking at a figure, understanding it right away, and seeing what is significant without having to look into the Results section.

Very important: explain in the caption EVERYTHING you see in the figure! X-axis indicates... Y-axis indicates... Blue dots indicate... Red line indicates... Error bars indicate... and so on.

Please divide the Method into the following parts (all in past tense!):

  1. Participants (who did the study? gender, age, how you recruited them, ethics etc)
  2. Material (the equipment (online exp or on site?), monitor, etc, how did you create and analyze the data?)
  3. Stimuli (what you showed on the screen, often a figure can clarify your stimuli)
  4. Procedure (the sequence of stimuli and tasks you showed on the screen — it is important to have a preliminary section called “Practice”, where you explain how the task was explained, if there was practice, etc. You can also have a subsection about Instructions.)
  5. Design (how many trials? blocks? how long was the exp? different groups? what did you test? what was the variable you were interested in?)
  6. Data Analysis (how did you analyze the results? What did you plot on the x and y-axis? Go step by step: first... second... third... Note that in the Results section you will have to quickly repeat what you did in 1-2 sentences.)

Discussion

Sometimes, in the flow of a section, you may have a list of things to report. It can be a list of possible mechanisms, a list of possible confounds, etc. Do not be afraid of using short sentences which start with FIRST… SECOND… THIRD… It clarifies the entire idea and makes it easier for the reader to follow.

Add a paragraph at the very beginning of your Discussion section, mentioning what you found. This part is exactly the sentence you would use when somebody asks: “What did you do your thesis on?” “What did you find?”

Important: much more credit is given to specific comments, limitations, and future directions than to general ones. A useful rule of thumb: if a comment could apply to many different studies, it is too general.

Examples of general comments to avoid:

  • The study was long, so participants' attention may have decreased over time.
  • The study was conducted in a lab rather than in a real-world setting.
  • The stimuli included only male faces.
  • The stimuli were grayscale faces and therefore not representative of everyday life.
  • The rating scale has inherent limitations.
  • The sample size was small and limited to university students.

These comments are not incorrect, but they are broad and could apply to many studies. Focus on points closely linked to the specific study — its design, rationale, and hypotheses. Referring to the literature will strengthen your answer.
